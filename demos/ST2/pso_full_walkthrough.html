Particle Swarm Optimisation — Full Walkthrough

The two equations — these are the ONLY things you compute

VELOCITY UPDATE: v(t+1) = w·v(t) + c1·r1·(pbest - x(t)) + c2·r2·(gbest - x(t)) POSITION UPDATE: x(t+1) = x(t) + v(t+1)

That's it. Two lines. Every PSO exam question reduces to these two equations.

What each variable means

VariableNameWhat it is
x(t)Current positionWhere the particle is right now
v(t)Current velocityHow fast and which direction it's moving
pbestPersonal bestBest position THIS particle has ever been at
gbestGlobal bestBest position ANY particle in the swarm has ever been at
wInertia weightHow much the current velocity carries over
c1Cognitive coefficientHow strongly the particle is pulled toward its own best
c2Social coefficientHow strongly the particle is pulled toward the swarm's best
r1, r2Random valuesBetween 0 and 1 — add randomness to the search

The three components of velocity

🔵 Inertia
w · v(t)

"Keep going in the same direction." Like a ball rolling — it doesn't stop instantly.

🟢 Cognitive
c1·r1·(pbest - x)

"Go back to the best place YOU found." Your personal memory pulling you back.

🟡 Social
c2·r2·(gbest - x)

"Go toward where the SWARM found the best." Following the crowd's best discovery.

If pbest > x(t), the cognitive term is positive (pulls right). If pbest < x(t), it's negative (pulls left). Same logic for social. The particle is always being tugged in two directions at once — its own memory vs the swarm's knowledge.

1
Compute the three velocity components separately
▼

Always break the velocity formula into 3 parts first. Much easier and you get method marks for each part.

Inertia
w · v(t)
= 0.8 × 2
= 1.6
Cognitive
c1·r1·(pbest-x)
= 2.0×0.5×(5-10)
= 1.0 × (-5)
= -5.0
Social
c2·r2·(gbest-x)
= 2.0×0.3×(12-10)
= 0.6 × 2
= 1.2
The cognitive term is NEGATIVE because pbest (5) is less than x (10) — the particle's personal best is to the LEFT of where it currently is, so it gets pulled left (negative direction). The social term is POSITIVE because gbest (12) is to the RIGHT of x (10).
2
Add the three components to get new velocity
▼
v(t+1) = inertia + cognitive + social v(t+1) = 1.6 + (-5.0) + 1.2 v(t+1) = 1.6 - 5.0 + 1.2 v(t+1) = -2.2
The result is negative — this means the particle will move LEFT (toward smaller values) on the next step. The strong cognitive pull toward pbest=5 (which is far left of x=10) dominates over the social pull toward gbest=12.
3
Compute new position
▼
x(t+1) = x(t) + v(t+1) x(t+1) = 10 + (-2.2) x(t+1) = 7.8
✓ New velocity = -2.2    New position = 7.8
The particle moved from 10 to 7.8 — it moved LEFT toward pbest=5, even though gbest=12 is to the right. This happened because c1·r1 (1.0) pulled harder than c2·r2 (0.6), and pbest was 5 units away while gbest was only 2 units away.
4
Update pbest and gbest? (what happens after position update)
▼

After moving to x=7.8, we evaluate the fitness of this new position:

If f(7.8) is better than f(pbest=5): → update pbest = 7.8 If f(7.8) is better than f(gbest=12): → update gbest = 7.8 Otherwise: → pbest and gbest stay the same
pbest and gbest are only ever updated to BETTER positions, never worse. They represent the best positions found so far — they don't reset each iteration.

Common exam mistakes

❌ Using v(t) in the position update instead of v(t+1)
You MUST compute the new velocity first, then use that new velocity to update position. Never do x(t+1) = x(t) + v(t).
❌ Getting the sign wrong on cognitive/social terms
If pbest < x, then (pbest - x) is NEGATIVE. If pbest > x, it's POSITIVE. Draw it on a number line if unsure.
❌ Saying PSO updates based on "average position" (Q8 from your past paper)
It's pbest AND gbest — not the average. You lost 2 marks on this in the past paper.
❌ Forgetting to multiply c1·r1 together before multiplying by (pbest-x)
The formula is c1 × r1 × (pbest - x), not c1 × (r1 × pbest - x).
✓ Always show your working in 3 separate lines — inertia, cognitive, social — then add them. Even if your final answer is wrong, you get method marks for each correct component.
✓ PSO is for continuous optimisation — particles have real-valued positions and velocities. It doesn't work naturally for discrete/binary problems like the Knapsack problem (that's GA's territory).
